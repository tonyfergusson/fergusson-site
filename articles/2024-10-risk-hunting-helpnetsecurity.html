Risk Hunting: A Proactive Approach to Cyber Threats

Tony Fergusson 30 Oct 2024 · Help Net Security
Article Help Net Security Risk Hunting Zero Trust

"We can only see a short distance ahead, but we can see plenty there that needs to be done." — Alan Turing

Cybersecurity today is an overly reactive industry. Too often we act like firefighters, rushing from blaze to blaze extinguishing flames hoping to keep the damage to a minimum, rather than fire suppression experts designing environments that refuse to burn.

Just consider the litany of Detect & Respond technologies advanced by analysts and enthusiastically purchased by enterprises over the past decade:

  • 2013 — Gartner coins the term Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)
  • 2016 — Managed Detection and Response (MDR) comes into use to emphasise the offloading of management responsibilities
  • 2018 — Extended Detection and Response (XDR) is introduced to convey the gathering of intel from multiple sources including EDR, NDR, SIEM, SOAR

Each of these technologies assumes the attacker is already in the environment. They are built on the premise of detecting a breach that has already happened, rather than preventing it. That is no longer sufficient given the speed and sophistication of today's threat landscape.

From Reactive to Proactive

Risk hunting is the proactive alternative. Rather than waiting for an indicator of compromise to appear in a SIEM dashboard, risk hunting focuses on mapping known tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) onto an organisation's existing defences — and using that understanding to close any gaps discovered before they can be exploited.

Unlike traditional threat hunting, which typically begins after an assumed breach, risk hunting begins with the question: "where are we exposed?" It is forward-looking by design.

Indicators of Attack, Not Just Compromise

The shift in mindset required is from Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) to Indicators of Attack (IOAs). IOCs tell you something has already gone wrong. IOAs — patterns and behaviours that indicate an ongoing or imminent attack — let security teams identify the TTPs used by threat actors before damage is done.

This distinction matters enormously. A security team equipped to recognise the early behavioural signals of a ransomware campaign has a fundamentally different posture from one that only responds once file encryption has begun.

Building the Risk Hunting Capability

Effective risk hunting requires bringing together complementary skills in a "purple team" approach — combining the offensive perspective of red teams (who actively probe for vulnerabilities) with the defensive knowledge of blue teams (who know the organisation's environment deeply). Neither capability alone is sufficient; together, they create a closed loop of continuous improvement.

AI and automation have a critical role to play in making this tractable. The volume of data generated by a modern enterprise environment is too large for human analysts to process manually. AI tools can surface patterns and anomalies that would otherwise be invisible, giving security teams actionable intelligence rather than an overwhelming stream of alerts.

Why Regulations Are Forcing This Shift

Regulations like NIS2 and DORA are deliberately designed to push organisations from reactive to proactive stances. NIS2 in particular mandates that organisations demonstrate they have assessed their risk posture and taken proportionate measures to address identified gaps — not just that they have deployed a set of security tools.

For security leaders, this is an opportunity. Risk hunting gives a concrete methodology to satisfy regulatory requirements while genuinely improving organisational resilience. It shifts the conversation with boards from "here is our incident response plan" to "here is how we are identifying and eliminating risk before incidents occur."

The organisations that will thrive in this environment are those that stop waiting for the fire alarm and start designing the fireproof building.
