Go Dark: Reduce Your Attack Surface

Tony Fergusson29 Jul 2021
ArticleZscaler

Most IT security professionals would agree that the key ingredient for safeguarding networks is "reducing the attack surface." Fewer avenues for breaches means reduced risk and fewer incidents for an enterprise: hackers can't attack what they can't see.

Reducing attack surface exposure really is the secret to securing your network, applications, and — most importantly — your data. I embraced the "reduce the attack surface" mantra back in 2015. Five years into my IT security journey, it is still my mission. I am more convinced than ever that it is critical to preventing network breaches — now and for the future.

The "Go Dark" Philosophy

The idea of making the internet or corporate network dark has gained momentum since the introduction of Software Defined Networking (SDN). This enabled a new concept called Software Defined Perimeter (SDP) — or what some are referring to as a Black Cloud.

The most important and fundamental principle of this mission is securing your enterprise by reducing the attack surface. This one principle will dramatically reduce the risk of being a victim of the next ransomware attack, or some data exfiltration campaign.

When an attacker scans your infrastructure, there is no answer-back. They cannot see what they cannot find. Traditional architectures expose vast swathes of your network to potential attackers — every open port, every VPN endpoint, every firewall interface is an invitation. Zero Trust Network Access (ZTNA) changes that equation entirely.

Reducing Attack Surface in Practice

At MAN Energy Solutions, we implemented this philosophy in production. "Everything is dark, so if there is an attacker and he scans my infrastructure, he won't see anything." We reduced the attack surface, which means there's no answer-back. But, at the same time, we still allow an engineer to connect to assets.

The approach enabled us to replace traditional approaches with a modern, secure, cloud-first implementation. With granular control over user permissions, each employee and contractor gets access to only what they need — nothing more.

The Market Guide for Zero Trust Network Access (Gartner, June 2020) validated this direction, and adoption has continued to accelerate. The lesson from WannaCry, NotPetya, and countless other incidents is consistent: organizations that had reduced their attack surface were far better positioned to withstand — and recover from — these events.

Lock down your network doors and windows with ZTNA. Go dark. Hackers cannot attack what they cannot see.
