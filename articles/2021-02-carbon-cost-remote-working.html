The Carbon Cost of Remote Working

Tony Fergusson26 Feb 2021
ArticleOpen Access Government

The shift to remote working during the pandemic has been heralded as a win for the environment — fewer cars on the road, fewer flights, less office energy consumption. And that's true as far as it goes. But as IT leaders, we need to examine the full picture, including the carbon emissions of the technologies organisations are deploying to enable remote working at scale.

The Hidden Carbon Cost of Legacy Infrastructure

Consider a legacy hardware appliance designed to filter incoming and outgoing web traffic — the kind typically used in large organisations. One year's operation of such an appliance requires approximately 49,000 megajoules of power, equating to 13,611 kilowatts of electricity, and produces 9.6 metric tons of carbon dioxide output.

To put that in perspective, this is a similar amount of CO₂ by-product as the consumption of 22.3 barrels of oil or burning 10,064 pounds of coal — the pollution of which requires dedicated photosynthetic efforts of more than 12.6 acres of trees to offset.

Multiply this across an enterprise estate of dozens or hundreds of such appliances, and the carbon math becomes alarming.

A Greener Path Forward

Thankfully, there are far more efficient, carbon-neutral ways of working that enable businesses to give their employees a greener way to work from home.

Within security, Secure Access Service Edge (SASE) implemented systems — which place security-processing in the cloud — mean security services are integrated as a service, with both incoming and outgoing data processed centrally. Rather than deploying and running power-hungry appliances in every office or data centre, organisations can leverage the economies of scale of major cloud providers, whose hyperscale data centres operate at dramatically higher efficiency ratings.

The organisations that will win on both security and sustainability are those willing to retire their legacy on-premises appliances and embrace cloud-native architectures. Remote working is an opportunity — not just for productivity and flexibility — but to fundamentally rethink our infrastructure's environmental footprint.

The question for every IT leader today is not just "are we secure?" but "are we sustainable?" The two goals, it turns out, point in the same direction.
