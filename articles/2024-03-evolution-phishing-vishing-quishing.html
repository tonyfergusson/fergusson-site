The Evolution of Phishing, Vishing and Quishing

Tony Fergusson 6 March 2024 · TechRadar Pro
Article TechRadar Threat Landscape Phishing

In its early stages, phishing attacks were often very simplistic and relied on impersonating reputable sources via written communication — emails and letters — to gain access to sensitive data. Now adversaries have adapted their techniques in the wake of the AI evolution. With the growing popularity of GenAI tools, voice-based phishing attacks — also known as "vishing" — have become the new norm, and organisations have to combat this evolution by modernising their IT security.

Phishing as the reconnaissance phase of a bigger attack

We have to look at the anatomy of an attack to understand the role that phishing is playing in the malware industry. While ransomware typically gets all the headlines once intruders monetise their efforts after successfully delivering a payload, there is less coverage on the overall infection cycle, which often starts with something as simple as phishing. The reconnaissance phase at the beginning of an attack plays an even more important role in the defence strategy.

When attackers are figuring out what an organisation's attack surface looks like, they use phishing as a mechanism to harvest confidential personal information such as credentials, or attempt to download zero-day malware to gain access to a particular machine. As adversaries use the latest trends like AI to trick users, organisations should put more focus on reducing their attack surface and applying advanced behavioural analysis mechanisms.

From phishing to vishing to quishing

The evolution has accelerated rapidly. Vishing (voice phishing) exploits AI-generated voices to create convincing impersonations of colleagues, executives, or service providers. With GenAI tools now capable of cloning a voice from a short audio sample, the attack surface has expanded dramatically. Victims receive calls that sound entirely authentic, asking them to transfer funds, reveal credentials, or take other actions an attacker desires.

Quishing — phishing via QR codes — has surged because it cleverly bypasses email security tools. Since the phishing link itself is an image rather than a text URL, it fails to trigger many email security solutions. Scanning the QR code with a phone (typically a personal device with weaker enterprise security) redirects the victim to a fraudulent site requesting sensitive information, or begins an immediate malware download.

The common thread across these evolutions is that attackers are deliberately using channels and formats that our existing defences weren't designed to catch. AI is accelerating this further, enabling more personalised and convincing attacks at scale.

Reducing the attack surface is the imperative

The solution is not simply better email filters. Organisations need to adopt a more holistic approach that starts with reducing the attack surface itself. If an attacker cannot reach a resource, the phishing attempt — however sophisticated — cannot succeed. Zero Trust architecture plays a critical role here: by ensuring users only ever have access to what they need, when they need it, and under continuous verification, the blast radius of any successful phishing attempt is dramatically reduced.

Advanced behavioural analysis is also essential. Rather than relying on signature-based detection of known phishing patterns, organisations should be looking for anomalous behaviour — unusual login times, unexpected data access, atypical device usage. These behavioural signals are far harder for attackers to mimic convincingly than the content of a phishing email.

Phishing is often just the beginning of the chain of compromise. By treating it as such — as the entry point to a much larger attack — security teams can build defences that address not just the initial lure but every subsequent step an attacker might take once inside the network.
