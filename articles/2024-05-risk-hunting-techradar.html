Risk Hunting: Unleashing the Proactive Force Inside Cybersecurity

Tony Fergusson 7 June 2024 · TechRadar Pro
Article TechRadar Risk Hunting Zero Trust

Today's threat landscape is larger than ever before and the speed at which an adversary can exploit gaps in a security framework is only getting faster. Faced with this dangerous combination, organisations are increasingly recognising the limitations of their more traditional reactive approaches to cybersecurity — the tactic of threat hunting. Typically, threat hunters look for an assumed breach or bad actors within their environment and try to mitigate the damage they can do.

However, the very definition of threat hunting suggests that the organisation has already been breached before any action is taken. That is no longer good enough. We need to shift the mindset of businesses from mitigating threats in progress to building a holistic overview of their environment that enables them to identify areas of risk ahead of time.

New regulations, such as NIS2 and DORA, are purposefully designed to make organisations more preventative and proactive in their cybersecurity approach — flipping to this new form of risk hunting instead. Far more proactive in its methodology, risk hunting empowers organisations to identify, assess, and mitigate potential risks before they manifest into concrete threats. But how should companies best go about implementing a risk hunting framework?

In what business areas should you be looking for risks?

As an industry we currently lack a proper understanding of how threat actors think and therefore what constitutes risk within an organisation. This is due to not anticipating the speed at which threats are evolving, which means that organisations are always defending against old attacks rather than anticipating their evolution. NIS2 — while helping to raise the bar for foundational security — is not detailed enough to close those risk gaps by itself. Companies need to build teams specifically designed to act as threat actors would, and test the limits of existing policies and frameworks.

Risk hunting shouldn't be focused solely on locating digital vulnerabilities. It needs to be as all-encompassing as possible — identifying how resilient the business might be if there is a DDoS attack or the internet goes down, for example.

What is the best approach to risk hunting?

There are numerous risk hunting methodologies, including advanced analytics, threat intelligence, and anomaly detection. Intelligence-based risk hunting is the optimum form from my perspective. Unlike threat hunting, which uses Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) and Tactics, Techniques and Procedures (TTPs) to determine where risk may lie, risk hunting needs to be using Indicators of Attack (IOA) — patterns or behaviours that indicate an ongoing or imminent attack. These indicators help identify the TTPs used by threat actors.

Many companies are testing digital simulations of potential attacks to understand how their security framework would stand up against attacks of different magnitudes. Security teams can then tweak their policies based on that intelligence. Where the gaps come in is in the skills needed to use the tools effectively and apply them in an already highly complex environment. Very few people have the inherent skill or knowledge to implement a risk hunting approach and understand what needs to be done to mitigate the dangers it identifies.

The best team for risk hunting is what we consider a "purple team" — a combination of red and blue team members. Red teams try to find vulnerabilities in an organisation's framework; blue teams close the gaps from within. Businesses don't need to hire completely new teams, but instead bring together disparate teams with a combined skill set to hunt internal risks from both a defence and offence perspective. These purple teams, supported by AI technologies, can ingest the right data and find the correct meaning from it to make the necessary changes.

Making risk hunting simple and actionable

Even with the right teams and tools in place, security teams still face an uphill battle understanding the risk data they collect. CISOs need to connect multiple tools into one solution framework that can connect the dots and quantify organisation-wide risk in a visual format. Data for data's sake isn't helpful to a security team with limited manpower and time — by using AI, teams can crunch the numbers and present a clear and actionable plan to present to the board.

For too long, businesses have been reactive to the cyber dangers that surround them. With the advent of NIS2 and DORA, security is going to be forced onto the agenda, giving security practitioners the stage to up-level their framework and push for greater investment. Risk hunting shouldn't stop with regulations — these audits should become part of an ongoing cycle for security teams to stay ahead of threat actors and ensure that their security framework is fit-for-purpose at all times.
